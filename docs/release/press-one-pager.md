## The pitch

You captain a hauler on the frontier rim. Each morning you roll a hand of d20 dice — that's
your day's luck, laid out in the open — and you decide where the good rolls go: the risky
smuggling run, or the getaway you might need later. Every other spacer in the galaxy runs on
the same dice, the same stats, and the same capacity for bad judgment. Fuel is scarce enough
that every jump, and every shot fired, is a commitment.

## Features

- **Everything runs on the d20.** The morning roll shows your luck before you spend it. The
  game never fudges — tension comes from allocation, not hidden odds.
- **The galaxy is a cast, not a backdrop.** Thirty-plus named NPCs, each with a full
  character sheet — stats, backstory, an Ideal, a Bond, and a Flaw that drives them to make
  _interesting_ choices instead of optimal ones. They trade, feud, gamble, get arrested, and
  take the contracts you turn down. The news wire reports what they did while you slept.
- **Fuel is the plot.** One resource gates travel, combat, and escape. "Can I afford to fire
  my guns this far from a depot?" is a question every encounter makes you ask.
- **Trading carries story.** Cargo isn't a price delta — it's a decision with a face on it:
  medicine for a plague station, a passenger who lies about her name, contraband a patrol
  captain will remember you for.
- **Tour One → veteran loop.** A tight first arc you can finish in one evening opens into an
  open-ended veteran career. Lose your ship and your successor inherits your maps, half your
  bank, and your reputation — good and bad. The galaxy remembers your name.
- **The demo carries in.** A full-length demo whose save continues directly into the full
  game — no restart.

## History

Spacer Quest began as a 1991 Apple II GBBS trading game by Firefox. In 2026 its original
source was located, decompiled, and faithfully reimplemented as a web app — then playtested
to the conclusion that the 1991 _design_ (menu ceremony, point grind) doesn't hold up, but
its _systems_ (scarce turns, fuel logistics, a living cast of rival spacers) are worth
building a real game on. _Rimward_ is that game: the original's systems, names, factions, and
tone survive; its hardware-era tedium does not.
