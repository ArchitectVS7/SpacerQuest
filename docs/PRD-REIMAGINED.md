# SPACER QUEST: RIMWARD
## Executive Product Requirements Document (Non-Technical)

**Version:** 0.1 — Visionary Draft
**Date:** July 2026
**Status:** Redesign phase. Supersedes the faithful-port PRD (`docs/PRD.md`) as design direction; the original PRD remains the canonical record of the 1991 rules and lore.
**Working title:** *Spacer Quest: Rimward* (placeholder — "Rimward" evokes the frontier pull of the original's rim systems).

---

## 1. Executive Summary

*Spacer Quest: Rimward* is a single-player space trading game with a fully committed retro-terminal aesthetic, built on the bones of the 1991 Apple II BBS game *Spacer Quest* — its star systems, factions, characters, and economy — but redesigned from first principles for how people actually play in 2026.

The pitch in one line: **FTL × Taipan × Citizen Sleeper, set in a galaxy full of D&D characters who play the game whether you're watching or not.**

Three ideas carry the game:

1. **Everything runs on the d20.** Each morning you roll a hand of dice — that's your day's luck, visible up front. You decide where the good rolls go: the risky smuggling run or the getaway you might need later. Every other spacer in the galaxy runs on the same dice, the same stats, and — crucially — the same capacity for bad judgment.
2. **The galaxy is a cast, not a backdrop.** Thirty-plus named NPCs each carry a full character sheet: stats, a backstory, an Ideal, a Bond, and a Flaw that mechanically drives them to make *interesting* choices instead of optimal ones. They trade, feud, gamble, get arrested, and take the contracts you turn down. The news wire reports what they did while you slept.
3. **Trading carries story.** Cargo isn't a price delta — it's a decision with a face on it. Medicine for a plague station, a passenger who lies about her name, contraband a patrol captain will remember you for. Fuel is scarce enough that every jump, and every shot fired, is a commitment.

The game launches as a premium title ($15–20, no microtransactions, ever) with a tight first-session arc, an open-ended veteran career behind it, and an expansion model built for drop-in content: new quest lines, new NPCs, new regions — the Rim, and eventually Andromeda, on the far side of the Nemesis black hole.

## 2. Goals of the Game

**Player-experience goals** — every design decision is tested against these five:

- **Every session tells a story you could retell.** Not "I made 40,000 credits" but "Rattlesnake jumped me at Deneb-4 with my tanks half-dry, and I bought him off with the spice crates I was supposed to deliver — and Cargo King has never let me forget it."
- **Scarcity makes decisions matter.** A handful of dice per day. Fuel that burns on every jump and every weapon discharge. A cargo hold that's always one crate too small. The player should never take an action idly.
- **The economy fights back.** No trade route stays optimal. Rival spacers take contracts you pass on, corner markets, buy the port out from under you. Era events — a blockade, a plague, a dilithium rush — re-price the map mid-career. The documented death of every trading game ("I found the loop, then I got bored") must never happen here.
- **Failure produces narrative, not repetition.** Lose your ship and your successor inherits your maps, half your bank, and your reputation — good and bad. The galaxy remembers your name.
- **Respect the player's time absolutely.** A meaningful session in 20 minutes. A full first arc in one evening. No grind, no filler travel, no menu ceremony. The BBS ancestor's one true modern insight — a limited number of turns per day — is kept; everything tedious about how it delivered them is discarded.

**Product goals:**

- Ship a demo whose median session exceeds one hour (the proven indie marketing engine — this is how Dome Keeper and its peers broke out).
- Sustain the game post-launch through content drops (Seasons), not monetization systems.
- Honor the 1991 original: its systems, names, factions, and tone survive; its hardware-era tedium does not.

## 3. Target Users

**Primary — "The Returner" (40–60).** Played Trade Wars 2002, Taipan, Elite, or Spacer Quest itself on a BBS or an Apple II. Has money, has nostalgia, has 45 minutes a night, and has been burned by retro revivals that faithfully reproduced the boredom. Wants the *feeling* back, not the friction. (This is a large, underserved, and vocal audience — the success of modern text-forward and terminal-aesthetic games proves they show up when the craft is real.)

**Secondary — "The Narrative Systems Player" (25–45).** Loves Citizen Sleeper, FTL, Sunless Sea, Wildermyth, Roadwarden. Plays for emergent stories and attachment to characters. Has never heard of a BBS and doesn't need to — for them this is a fresh dice-driven trading RPG with a striking look.

**Tertiary — "The Coffee-Break Strategist."** Plays Slipways, Dome Keeper, Balatro. Wants one dense, complete decision-loop per sitting. Served by the day-based structure: a day of Spacer Quest is a complete unit of play.

## 4. Differentiators

1. **A d20 society, not an AI backdrop.** No competitor in the trading genre runs its entire NPC population on transparent tabletop-RPG logic — sheets, rolls, and flaws the player can learn and exploit. Watching Wild Card blow a fortune at the Spacers Hangout because he failed a Flaw check isn't scripted flavor; it's the simulation, and it was equally likely to happen to *you*.
2. **The dice are honest and visible.** The morning roll shows you your luck before you spend it. The game never fudges. Tension comes from allocation, not from hidden odds — the player always knows what they risked and why they lost.
3. **Fuel is the plot.** One resource gates travel, combat, and escape. "Can I afford to fire my guns this far from a depot?" is a question no other trading game makes you ask on every encounter. (This mechanic was discovered, not invented — our own automated playtesting revealed it as the most interesting tension in the original's restored rules.)
4. **Terminal aesthetic as instrument, not wallpaper.** One screen — a diegetic ship's computer: starmap, manifest, fuel gauge, news wire — rendered in committed amber-phosphor CRT style with modern typography, animation, and sound (relay clicks, drive hum, the radio crackle of an incoming contract). Duskers-grade commitment, not scanline shader on a menu.
5. **A living lineage.** This is a real 1991 BBS game, rediscovered and reimagined by someone who waited thirty years to play it. The authentic roster, systems, factions, and formulas survive inside a modern design. That story is the marketing hook no competitor can copy.
6. **Premium and finished, with room to grow.** No microtransactions, no energy timers, no season passes. Expansions are content: new quest lines and NPCs dropped into the veteran loop.

## 5. The Object of the Game

### 5.1 Tour One — the first session (60–120 minutes)

You are a nobody with a junker ship and a 25,000-credit debt to the Merchant Guild of Sun-3, due in 30 days. The object is simple and stated on the first screen: **clear the debt and make your name.**

Tour One is a complete, authored arc that doubles as the tutorial. It introduces the dice, the manifest board, fuel logistics, the first handful of NPCs, and one full combat. It ends on a decisive Day-30 beat: debt cleared (or not), your first rank earned, and a hook — a fragment of a signal that shouldn't exist, sold to you by the Wise One of Polaris-1 — that opens the veteran game.

### 5.2 The Veteran Loop — the open career

After Tour One, your spacer persists. The frontier opens into an ongoing career with three interleaved objects:

- **Climb the Registry.** The original's nine ranks — Lieutenant through Giga Hero, with **Conqueror** as the career capstone — return as the Renown ladder, earned through Deeds (§8.2), not point grinding.
- **Pick your side.** Four alliance arcs (§8.1) — Astro League, Space Dragons, Warlord Confederation, Rebel Alliance — each a full questline with a distinct playstyle. Committing to one changes how the other three treat you.
- **Chase the Signal.** The main mystery (§8.1) accumulates across the whole career: fragments of knowledge about the Nemesis black hole, gathered from storylets, ruins, and the Sage of Mizar-9. It culminates in the game's ultimate gamble — a one-way crossing to Andromeda, attempted only when you're willing to bet everything you've built.

**Death is legacy, not game over.** Lose your ship for good and your successor takes the license: your charts, half your bank, your Deeds, and your reputation with every NPC who knew you — grudges included.

### 5.3 Expansion — the drop-in model

The veteran loop is deliberately built as a *content socket*. Because NPCs are data (a sheet, a backstory, hooks) and quests are storylet chains, expansion Seasons drop new material into a running career without resets: a new roster of rivals, a new alliance crisis, a new region (the Rim systems, then Andromeda itself as the far-future expansion — a second frontier with its own cast for players who made the crossing). Each Season also re-prices the world (an era event), guaranteeing veteran economies never settle.

## 6. The Living Galaxy — NPCs as Characters

Every named spacer in the galaxy — launch target: **~30 at start, drawn from the original's roster and the twenty personalities already designed** (Silk Dagger, Cargo King, Iron Vex, Lucky Seven, Rattlesnake, Doc Salvage, Wild Card, Stellar Monk...) — carries the same character sheet the player does:

**Five stats** (each -2 to +5, modifying d20 rolls):

| Stat | Governs |
|---|---|
| **PILOT** | flying, evasion, navigation, the getaway |
| **GUNS** | weapons, boarding, intimidation |
| **TRADE** | bargaining, appraisal, reading a market |
| **GRIT** | endurance, repairs, holding nerve under fire |
| **GUILE** | smuggling, deception, gambling, reading people |

**Plus three roleplay hooks, straight from the 5e playbook, but mechanical:**

- **Ideal** — what they're for. Steers strategy. *(Stellar Monk: "Wealth is ballast. I fly light.")*
- **Bond** — who they're for. Creates obligations the simulation honors. *(Doc Salvage: "I never leave a mayday unanswered — I was left once.")*
- **Flaw** — the crack in the armor. **Flaws override optimal play.** When a decision touches an NPC's flaw, they roll against it; on a failure, the flaw chooses. *(Rattlesnake: "I never let an insult go. Never." — which is how a Tier-2 pirate ends up attacking a Warlord convoy, and making headlines.)*

**How they decide.** On each simulation tick, every NPC picks among the same verbs the player has — trade, travel, fight, gamble, socialize, lay low — weighted by Ideal and situation, then rolls d20 + stat to execute. The margin decides how well it goes. A natural 20 or natural 1 always generates a story, and stories go out on the **Galactic News Wire**: the scrolling feed on your ship's terminal that doubles as the game's heartbeat. *"WIRE: Lucky Seven wins the Fat Profit off Cargo King in a Spacer's Dare at the Hangout. Cargo King unavailable for comment."*

**Power tiers.** NPCs span five tiers, from Tier-1 mudlarks flying patched freighters to Tier-5 legends (the Admiral Sterns of the galaxy). Encounter matchmaking respects tiers — but flaws don't, which is how low-tier spacers punch up, die famously, or get very lucky. The original's 65-entry combat roster (pirates, patrols, brigands, Reptiloids) is redistributed across these tiers as the anonymous rank-and-file beneath the named cast.

**They remember.** Reputation is per-NPC, not just per-faction. Bail Silk Dagger out of the Regulus-6 brig and she owes you — the simulation will pay that debt at the worst possible moment for her and the best possible moment for you. Sell out Smuggler Ray to a patrol and every Rebel-aligned spacer's disposition shifts.

## 7. The Core Game Loop

A career is made of **days**. A day is a complete, satisfying unit of play — five to ten minutes — and it always has the same shape:

**1. DAWN — roll the day.** Your terminal boots. You roll your hand of d20s — typically five — and see them all at once. `[17] [12] [9] [4] [2]` is a very different day from `[19] [18] [16] [11] [8]`, and you know it before you commit to anything. (Ship upgrades and crew can add dice, allow one re-roll, or set a floor.)

**2. THE WIRE — read the galaxy.** The news feed tells you what the cast did overnight: prices shifting, feuds escalating, a bounty posted, a friend in jail. This is both flavor and intelligence — the wire is where opportunities surface.

**3. THE DAY — spend your dice.** Every meaningful action consumes one die of your choice, its value + your stat vs. a visible difficulty. Jump to another system. Sign a manifest contract. Haggle. Explore a beacon. Visit the Hangout. Fight — combat consumes dice *and* fuel. When the hand is empty, the day ends.

**4. DUSK — the world moves.** Every NPC takes their day. Markets tick. Consequences land. The next morning's wire is written by what everyone — including you — just did.

The elegance this buys: **the player and the galaxy run on one system.** There is no separate "AI." When Iron Vex misses his attack run, he rolled low on the same die you roll. The game is a table, and everyone at it is playing.

### 7.1 Sample Turn — TRADE

*Day 12. Docked at Vega-6. Hand: [17] [12] [9] [4] [2].*

The manifest board posts four contracts. Two are plain freight. The third pays triple: **Medicinals to Fomalhaut-2**, flagged URGENT — the wire explained why this morning: *"Fever outbreak in Fomalhaut-2 orbital district; Governor appeals for aid."* The fourth is quietly excellent money for **Dry Goods to Mira-9** — except you notice the broker is one of Black Tide's shell outfits, and questions are discouraged.

You take the Medicinals. The haggle is a TRADE check against the broker — you spend the **[12]**, add your TRADE +2, clear the DC, and squeeze out a hazard bonus. The **[17]** you're saving: Fomalhaut-2 is two jumps out and the wire mentioned Rim pirates working the Deneb lane. Fuel math: two jumps costs 240 units; you're carrying 300. Enough to get there — not enough to get there *and* fight. You spend the **[4]** topping off at Vega's depot while prices are decent.

*One die left. The Hangout is loud tonight, and Lucky Seven is dealing Spacer's Dare...*

**What just happened invisibly:** the plague event re-priced a region; a rival (Black Tide) surfaced through the economy rather than a cutscene; the player made a values choice (aid vs. dirty money) that both pay in credits but differently in reputation; and fuel turned a route decision into a combat decision.

### 7.2 Sample Turn — EXPLORATION

*Day 19. Deep space, off the lane between Arcturus-6 and the Rim. Hand: [15] [11] [10] [7] [3].*

Yesterday's passenger — the one who lied about her name — paid her fare in coordinates: a derelict beacon off the charts. Reaching it burns 80 fuel and a die for the navigation check. You spend the **[10]** + PILOT +1 and thread it.

The beacon is pre-Confederation, still transmitting. A GRIT check — **[15]**, easily — gets the airlock open without tearing your hull. Inside: salvage (real credits), a sealed cargo pod (Contraband — carrying it is a choice, and patrol captains roll GUILE checks against smugglers), and the actual treasure: a **Signal Fragment**, the third you've found. Your terminal's Nemesis file updates. The Sage of Mizar-9 will talk about this one for free — the Sage always talks when you bring something *new*.

You burn the **[3]** on a shipwide sweep before you leave — a low roll, and it goes badly: you trip a dormant defense drone. It's Tier-1 junk, but you're 400 fuel from the nearest depot, and every shot costs 50...

**What just happened:** exploration feeds all three career objects at once — money, the main mystery, and a smuggling temptation — and the fuel-combat trap sets up the next vignette. Knowledge (the Fragment) is a progression currency that survives even death.

### 7.3 Sample Turn — ALLIES

*Day 23. The Spacers Hangout, Sun-3. Hand: [18] [13] [8] [6] [5].*

The wire, this morning: *"Doc Salvage impounded at Regulus-6 — unpaid landing fees, ship seized."* Doc is Tier-2, harmless, beloved, and the only spacer with the parts network to fix an Astraxial hull. You've never worked together. That's the point.

Bail is 4,000 credits — payable remotely via a broker, no roll required, just money you were saving for a shield tier. Or, tonight, Admiral Stern (Tier 5, Astro League, Ideal: *"Order is worth any price"*) is holding court in the Hangout's back room, and a GUILE check could persuade the Admiral that impounding rescue pilots is bad for League recruitment — making the *League* pay Doc's bail, and putting you on Stern's radar in both good ways and bad.

You spend the **[18]** + GUILE +1 on the Admiral. Clear it. The wire tomorrow: *"Astro League intervenes in Regulus-6 impound; Doc Salvage released, credits League goodwill fund."* Doc's Bond — *"I never leave a mayday unanswered"* — is now pointed at **you**. Permanently. The simulation will honor it: someday, when your hull is venting and your dice are garbage, Patchwork will answer.

**What just happened:** relationships are investments with mechanical payoff; there were two prices (money vs. dice + risk) for the same outcome; and a Tier-5 NPC's Ideal was a *door*, because sheets are legible and learnable.

### 7.4 Sample Turn — COMBAT

*Day 27. Two jumps from Fomalhaut-2, the Medicinals still in the hold. Hand: [16] [14] [9] [5] [2]. Fuel: 210.*

Contact. **Rattlesnake** — Tier 3, GUNS +3, and his sheet, which you've learned the hard way, reads: *Flaw — "I never let an insult go."* Three weeks ago, at the Hangout, you laughed at his hand of Spacer's Dare.

Combat is rounds; each round you commit a die and a stance:

- **Round 1 — you run.** PILOT check, your **[14]** +1 vs. his pursuit roll. He rolls better. The Fang closes, and his opening volley takes your shields to half. Each round of running burns fuel too — you're down to 180.
- **Round 2 — you talk.** Tribute is the old game's mechanic, kept and sharpened: buying him off is a TRADE check, cheap if you offer early, escalating every round. But his Flaw makes the DC brutal — *this is personal.* You don't spend a good die on a bad bet.
- **Round 3 — you fight.** The **[16]** + GUNS +2, and 50 fuel to fire — leaving 130, right at the edge of what gets you to Fomalhaut-2. Your volley beats his GRIT roll and cracks the Fang's drive. His retreat check — he rolls a natural 20, and the wire will call it *"Rattlesnake's miracle burn at Deneb-4."* He escapes. He'll heal. He'll remember.

You dock at Fomalhaut-2 on fumes, deliver the Medicinals into the outbreak, and the Governor's gratitude goes out on the wire. **Deed unlocked: Mercy Runner.** Renown climbs. Somewhere, Rattlesnake reads the headline.

**What just happened:** combat was five decisions, not a dice-off — run, talk, or fight, which die, and *whether you can afford the fuel to shoot at all.* An NPC flaw closed one door (tribute) and the player's own history opened the fight. Nobody died; the story compounds instead.

### 7.5 Sample Turn — A BAD DAY

*Day 30. Debt due. 22,400 of 25,000 banked. Hand: [7] [5] [4] [3] [2].*

The dice are a disaster and you can see it at dawn — which means you can *plan* it. No checks today that a smart route can avoid: no combat lanes, no haggling with sharps. The one sure thing on the board is a short, dull freight run, Sun-3 to Spica-3, DC so low even a **[5]** clears it. It pays 2,100. Not enough.

The gap is 500. Options: the Hangout (Spacer's Dare, GUILE, terrible dice — a mug's game tonight); a quiet word with Penny Wise, who lends at rates that become their own quest line; or the sealed Contraband pod still bolted in your hold from Day 19, which Smuggler Ray would buy tonight, no roll needed — for a price that solves everything and a reputation flag that never fully washes out.

There is no correct answer. That's the design.

**What just happened:** low rolls create a *different* game, not a worse one — the bad-day skill is routing around checks entirely; and the game's pressure produces values decisions right on schedule.

## 8. Quests, Achievements, and Side Stories

### 8.1 Quest lines — three layers

- **The main arc: *The Nemesis Signal.*** A career-long mystery told in fragments — found in derelicts, bought from the Wise One, decoded by the Sage, and occasionally traded by NPCs who don't know what they have. It asks one question: *what is broadcasting from the far side of the black hole?* Fragments are knowledge, and knowledge is the one currency death never takes. The arc ends at the event horizon, with everything you own on the table. (Andromeda itself is the expansion.)
- **Alliance arcs — four ways to play the same galaxy.** Each faction offers a full questline that doubles as a playstyle: the **Astro League** (law, patrol contracts, order at any price), the **Space Dragons** (honor, the dueling circuit, strength as currency), the **Warlord Confederation** (conquest, port ownership, tribute empires), and the **Rebel Alliance** (free trade, smuggling lanes, the frontier ungoverned). Rising in one reshapes prices, patrol behavior, and NPC dispositions across all four. Veterans can betray, defect, and pay for it.
- **NPC personal chains.** Every named spacer carries a two-to-four-episode personal story keyed to their Bond and Flaw — findable only by getting close to them. Help Silk Dagger settle the debt behind her name. Learn why Stellar Monk flies empty. Decide whether Wild Card's big score is worth co-signing. Because NPCs act autonomously, **chains can resolve without you** — ignore Doc Salvage's impound long enough and the wire will report how it ended. The galaxy does not wait, and that is why it feels alive.

### 8.2 Achievements — the Registry of Deeds

No gamerscore popups. Achievements are **Deeds** — in-world entries in the Spacer's Registry, written in period voice (*"Mercy Runner — broke the Fomalhaut fever blockade, Day 27 of the Frontier Era"*). Deeds are the game's progression spine: they grant Renown (the rank ladder is climbed by *doing things*, not by point accumulation — the direct fix for the original's fatal grind), they unlock storylets and titles, and they persist across successors. A veteran's Registry page is their career's autobiography, and the natural thing players screenshot and share.

### 8.3 Side stories — storylets everywhere

Short, authored micro-fictions (a screen or two, one decision) attached to the fabric of play: to cargo (the crate that ticks), to passengers (the woman with the false name), to ports (Algol-2 has no repair shop, and there's a reason), to the Hangout's rumor table, and to the Wise One and Sage — the original's trivia hermits reborn as the mystery's knowledge brokers. Launch target: **60–80 storylets**, each written so the *economy* delivers it (a contract, a price spike, a wire item) rather than a quest marker. Seasons add more in batches.

## 9. What We Keep From 1991

The redesign is a transformation, not an erasure. Surviving intact: the map (14 core systems, 6 Rim, Andromeda beyond), the four alliances, the cargo economy and its manifest-contract structure, fuel arbitrage, tribute, salvage, the Spacers Hangout, the Wise One and the Sage, ports as purchasable property, the nine ranks and the Conqueror capstone, the Reptiloids, the black hole at Nemesis — and the balance mathematics, which have already survived a modern automated playtest campaign. The twenty bot personalities become the founding cast. The original's soul — *scarce turns, a fragile ship, one more run to the rim* — is not just kept; it is finally the whole point.

What does not survive: the 54-screen menu labyrinth, the session/trip click tax, the point-grind win condition, and every place where 1991's hardware — not its designer — made the rules.

## 10. Out of Scope (v1)

- Multiplayer of any kind. The async arena (post your ship, rivals fight it while you're away) is the strongest original idea for a future Season — parked deliberately.
- Andromeda as playable space (the crossing is v1's ending; the far side is an expansion).
- 3D, real-time flight, or any departure from the terminal-cockpit presentation.
- Monetization beyond premium purchase + paid expansions. No exceptions.
